Holo3.1: Fast & Local Computer Use Agents

HuggingFace Blog / H Company · June 2, 2026

A family of open-weight computer-use models (0.8B → 35B-A3B) that runs locally — including FP8, NVFP4, and Q4 GGUF checkpoints. The 35B model hits 79.3% on AndroidWorld and 25%+ better than its predecessor inside third-party agent harnesses. On a DGX Spark, NVFP4 with agent-harness tuning cuts average step time from 6.8s to 3.3s. Supports web, desktop, and mobile GUI environments and integrates via function-calling or structured JSON into any agent stack.

Why you'll like it: The story here is specifically local, private deployment: the models run entirely on-device (consumer Apple Silicon or a local Spark), nothing leaves your network. That's the "no API dependency" path for building agent products with computer-use capability — the architecture (multiple sizes, quantized, any framework) is designed precisely so you can wire it into a product stack without locking into a hosted provider.

Claude Opus 4.8: "a modest but tangible improvement"

Simon Willison's Weblog · May 28, 2026

Willison's notes on the Claude Opus 4.8 release. The headline improvement for builders: mid-conversation system messages — you can now inject a role: "system" mid-loop without restating the full system prompt, preserving prompt-cache hits on earlier turns and cutting input cost on long agentic sessions. The minimum cacheable prompt also dropped from 4,096 to 1,024 tokens. On the model side: 4× less likely to let code flaws pass unremarked, and the system card shows it abstains rather than hallucinating on uncertain questions.

Why you'll like it: The mid-conversation system message feature is directly useful in agentic loops that need to update instructions based on tool results without blowing the cache — exactly the scenario that gets expensive in multi-step agents. Willison also notes Anthropic's unusual move of honestly labeling this "a modest but tangible improvement," which itself signals something about where the lab is in its release cadence.

If You Are Asking for Human Attention, Demonstrate Human Effort

Tom Bedor's Blog · June 11, 2026 · 1,489 pts, 458 comments on HN

A two-minute essay on the new team etiquette problem nobody has named yet: as AI output floods collaboration, the implicit contract around reading each other's work has quietly changed. The argument: when you forward unreviewed AI output to a colleague, you're asking them to spend attention on something you wouldn't spend attention on yourself. Bedor's rule — "if you're requesting human attention, demonstrate human effort" — means always labeling AI-generated content, always adding your own commentary, and always reviewing code before asking for a human review.

Why you'll like it: This is the thread that took off this week because it names something that's been accumulating friction in every engineering team. The 458-comment HN discussion goes much deeper: when is AI output "yours," what does code review even mean anymore, and whether the rule Bedor proposes is right or subtly off. Worth reading the post and at least the top-voted comments.

I Am Not a Reverse Centaur (HN discussion)

Hacker News · via miguelgrinberg.com · June 12, 2026 · 248 pts, 177 comments

Miguel Grinberg — Flask author, longtime open-source maintainer — uses Cory Doctorow's term "reverse centaur" (a human being puppeteered by a machine, doing its mechanical bidding) and announces he will not become one. Practically: LLMs are banned from all his repos. He's rejecting the pull-request flood of AI-generated contributions pushing him to spend more time reviewing machine output than he'd spend doing the work himself. The 177-comment HN thread breaks into two camps: people who agree it's a legitimate form of project governance, and people who think he's throwing away good contributions on principle.

Why you'll like it: This is the counterpoint to the agentic-economy narrative — a credible, experienced maintainer drawing a line and explaining why. Artnet News · April 23, 2026 · opens June 20, 2026 — Los Angeles

The world's first AI art museum — five galleries inside Frank Gehry's Grand L.A. complex, opening June 20. The inaugural show, "Machine Dreams: Rainforest," runs ecological data (birdsongs, plant patterns, weather measurements) from the Smithsonian, London's Natural History Museum, and the Cornell Lab through Anadol's open-source Large Nature Model, generating what he calls "digital sculptures" of alternate possible rainforests. To Access Your Creativity, Start Playing

Big Think · adapted from Prioritize Play by Rachael Renae · June 9, 2026

The core argument: adulthood systematically trains you to value productivity over play, but play is not the opposite of work — it's the gateway to genuine creative output. Renae draws on developmental psychology and adult creativity research to argue that play creates the psychological safety to experiment, fail, and try odd combinations of ideas without judgment. The piece distinguishes productive play (structured experiments with low stakes) from pure entertainment, and explains why the "I'll play when I have more time" logic is self-defeating: play isn't the reward for productive work, it's the precondition for it.

Why you'll like it: This lands in the constraints/creativity theme you've been thinking about — but the specific mechanism here is psychological safety rather than restriction. The argument that you have to build the habit of low-stakes experimentation deliberately (not wait for inspiration) is directly relevant when you're building toward a version of yourself that's also a founder. AI Creativity and the Instant Imitator Trap

Tom Bedor's Blog · April 15, 2026

Starting from Morris Stein's 1959 definition of creativity (a work must be original, effective, and accepted by an audience), Bedor argues that AI faces a structural problem: any original AI work can be instantly replicated by any other AI, so audience recognition of the original is impossible. He calls this the "instant imitator trap." Human artists benefit from a time gap — imitators need to learn and publish — which lets reputation solidify. AI collapses that gap to zero. His conclusion, though, is optimistic: this is why human creativity still matters and why Sora's death was predictable. Includes diagrams that make the argument unusually clear.

Why you'll like it: This is the kind of post that reframes a question you thought was settled ("can AI be creative?") into a more interesting one ("what does audience recognition require, and can AI ever have it?"). The structural argument — not about model quality but about the economics of imitation and recognition — is genuinely non-obvious, and the optimistic conclusion (human creativity is structurally protected by the imitator gap) is worth having in your head when you're building things at the art-tech intersection.
